VR3S: VR surgical simulation suite

Role

One of two designers, overseen by a creative director.

Tools

Figma, Miro, Jira

Overview & Impact

Our client wanted our help to build a VR multiplayer workspace shared by doctors across the globe, to accelerate knowledge sharing and increase collaboration between hospitals. As an alpha MVP was already built, I focused primarily on multiplayer interactions, multi-user roles and permissions, lobby management, and expanding the UI system to encompass a desktop experience.

As of August 2025, VR3S (Virtual Reality Surgical Simulation Suite) has been:

  • Used for 500+ complex congenital heart defect patients
  • Deployed at 3 USA hospitals and 4 internationally in Asia, South America, and the Middle-East, with more to come

Background

As the top children’s hospitals in the USA, Cincinnati Children’s Hospital and Medical Center (CCHMC) has many colleagues around the world seeking their guidance around complex patient cases. Our client’s specialization, congenital heart surgery, is one of the most complex surgical specialities in the world, with only about 50 doctors in the USA able to perform such surgeries. These doctors operate on a heart the size of a walnut, in babies from a few weeks to a few months old.

Envisioning a collaborative, virtual world

Our 4-day envisioning with the client left us with a few goals:

  1. Create an application that supports multiple organizations and users, with user authentication.
  2. Allow private sessions with multiple users accessed via invite.
  3. Build VR collaboration tools that allow seamlessly transferring of presenter lead between users.
  4. Enable voice and text communication such as annotations and labels.

In order to synthesize these goals with potential features and scoping, I organized 700+ notes and diagrams from the envisioning into design and technical requirements, which the team used to prioritize features.

Spreadsheet of role permissions across admins, organizations, case owners, and regular users.

Managing users and organizations

One of our largest challenges was organization management and user roles. As patient cases could involve private information, we needed a way to create organizations for colleagues to work together, as well as invite-only, cross-organization sharing to allow for borderless communication.

I conducted research across several file-sharing platforms to determine the potential actions required for each user level. I created a table outlining these actions, creating permissions for organization management, user management, case file management, session management, and user roles within the session (ie. presenters, collaborators).

Spreadsheet of role permissions across admins, organizations, case owners, and regular users.

Prioritizing features and preventing scope-creep

Throughout the project, I lead design presentations to the client on a bi-weekly basis, to ensure we were on track and could tackle any scope-creep as quickly as possible.

One of these trade-offs was creating UI that required lower developer effort, so we could focus more of our time on the features themselves. Thus, I created a robust and simple design library with scalable buttons, containers, and repeated colours where possible.
